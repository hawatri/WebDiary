Demystifying Quantum Concepts

An exploration of Fourier transforms and the Dirac delta function.

The Fourier Transform: From $\psi$ to $\psi^*$

In quantum mechanics, the wavefunction $\psi(x)$ describes a particle in position space. The Fourier transform allows us to see the same particle in momentum space, represented by $\tilde{\psi}(k)$. It's like looking at the same object from two different but equally valid perspectives.

Wavefunction $\psi(x)$

The inverse Fourier transform reconstructs the wavefunction from its momentum components:

$$\psi(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \tilde{\psi}(k) e^{ikx} dk$$

Complex Conjugate $\psi^*(x)$

To find the representation for the complex conjugate, $\psi^*(x)$, we take the conjugate of the entire expression. The key change happens in the complex exponential, where $(e^{ikx})^* = e^{-ikx}$. This leads to:

$$\psi^*(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \tilde{\psi}^*(q) e^{-iqx} dq$$

Why use 'q' instead of 'k'? The variable `q` is just a "dummy variable" for integration, representing the wave number just like `k`. We use a different letter to avoid confusion when both $\psi(x)$ and $\psi^*(x)$ appear in the same larger equation, ensuring the two integrals are treated as mathematically distinct.

The Enigmatic Dirac Delta Function

The Dirac delta function, $\delta(x)$, is not a traditional function. It's a "generalized function" or "distribution." You can think of it as an infinitely high, infinitesimally narrow spike at $x=0$, whose total area is exactly 1. Its true meaning, however, is revealed by how it behaves inside an integral.

Equality for Distributions

We can't just ask if two distributions are equal at every point. Instead, we say two distributions, $D_1(x)$ and $D_2(x)$, are equal if they produce the same result when integrated against any arbitrary "test function" $f(x)$:

$$D_1(x) = D_2(x) \iff \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} D_1(x)f(x)dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} D_2(x)f(x)dx$$

Proving that $x\delta(x) = 0$

This surprising result is a direct consequence of the rule above. We test both sides by integrating with $f(x)$:

  • Left-Hand Side: We use the "sifting property" of the delta function, which picks out the value of the function it's multiplied by at $x=0$.
    $$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} [x\delta(x)] f(x) dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(x) [xf(x)] dx = [xf(x)]_{x=0} = 0 \cdot f(0) = 0$$
  • Right-Hand Side: This is much simpler.
    $$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} [0] f(x) dx = 0$$

Since both sides of the equation yield 0 when integrated, we conclude that the distributions themselves are equal: $x\delta(x) = 0$.
